## The Process
Since I was new to traffic analysis, I had to watch tutorials to figure out where to start. I always thought the traffic analysis was wizardry, but it's really just log analysis once you understand the protocols and how they operate.

## My attempts:
### Attempt 1/3:
On my first attempt, I answered all the questions to the best of my ability without guessing on any of the questions and getting an answer wheater it was right or wrong. I ended up getting 6 questions wrong out of  29, which is 79.31%. I looked at my notes and studied hard with the new idea that exactly 6 questions are wrong. I took a little less than 2 weeks before starting my next attempt, only after I was confident I had found the 6 wrong answers.
* **23/29**
* **11 hours 40 mins**
* **Top 100 at that time**

### Attempt 2/3:
On my second attempt, I had already figured out which 6 answers I believed were wrong. However, I already had an idea the second I found out that rankings were based on score and time. If I could get a faster time than everyone, then I could get a really good ranking. So I used this second attempt to create an automation to input my answers faster than I ever could.

### Building the automation
My first idea was to just send the HTTP submit request with the correct answer offsets/values. I noticed that the answers' positions were randomized, so I would have to actually read the questions and answer choices to select the correct one. My second idea was to hack the timer functionality to pause or manipulate my time for better results; however, I wasn't sure if this would be a violation, and I also couldn't find any vulnerabilities. So I decided to create a bot using JavaScript to read HTML elements quickly and select the answers as they appear in a dictionary that I defined with the answers I believe are correct. Unfortunately, when creating the automation, it accidentally submitted it, but THANKFULLY it didn't start the last attempt I had.
* **28/29**
* **7 hours 21 mins**
* **Top 30**

### Attempt 3/3 (Last Chance):
So the bot had accidentally submitted the test while working on it, which isn't good, and even worse 1 of my answers is apparently wrong. I finished working on the bot, and everything logic-wise looked fine, but I didn't have any other chances to test it. I was now stuck between 2 choices: 1. I could try to fix my wrong answer and then run the automation and possibly get a 100% with a fast time, or 2. Keep my current answers and guarantee a ranking increase because if I change an answer that wasn't the single answer I got wrong initially, I would get 27/29, meaning my ranking would stay the same.

### My decision and realization
I decided I was going to strive for the highest score I could with the fastest time; it was all or nothing. So I studied my notes and reevaluated my answers, looking at them as if they all were wrong, but I couldn't prove any of my answers wrong, not even a little bit. And that's when I checked my code for the bot with my notes on what I believed the answers were. That's when I saw it, one of the questions in the code was different than what I had in my notes the whole time. I was upset and relieved at the same time. I figured out the wrong and now just had to believe in my code.

### The submission
If I had mistyped anything in the code, it wouldn't select the correct answer. If the website changed the HTML in an unexpected way, my program would fail. If there is a logic error that doesn't allow my code to continue, time would be wasted. Everything had to be perfect with failsafes in place to ensure speed.
